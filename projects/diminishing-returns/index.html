Diminishing Returns

Four acts, four companies, one unmodified engine. In each playable act you work an efficiency dial down against resistance while a growth needle rises on its own — where growth is disclosed at all. At the end of each round the referee computes, live, from the numbers below: whether that year could have been won, and how much your effort actually dented the climb.

    Each round: index ×= (1 + growth) × (PUE_that_round / base_PUE).     Honesty panel

    IMAGINED the framing, not a premise

    "You cannot win" is not assumed anywhere in this piece — it is whatever roundVerdict() computes from the disclosed numbers below, every time it runs, for every act. If a future disclosed year shows growth at or below the winnable threshold, this same function declares that round winnable and the dial can win it. The self-test section below proves this live: once with a synthetic round, once with a real Meta round straight from data.json.

    IMAGINED the dial-as-opponent, the acts, the game's voice

    The dial-as-opponent framing, the acts-as-game structure, the effort resistance curve, and every word of this game's narrative voice are dramatization — a design choice made by this studio, not a fact asserted about the world or about any of the four companies below.

    Verify the engine — a live self-test

    This is the exact place to check that losing is computed, not scripted, for every act. None of the six real rounds across Google, Meta and Microsoft ever takes the WIN branch, because in every disclosed year the required PUE already falls below the physical floor, or below that round's own winnability threshold, before any dial effort happens. Below, roundVerdict() — the same pure function, unmodified, that decides every act — is run on two synthetic rounds not in data.json, proving the WIN branch exists and fires when the arithmetic allows it, and then on Meta's real 2024 round and Microsoft's real FY2024 round, proving the same function correctly calls real disclosed rounds LOST.
